Strong Quake Rattles Baguio, Residents Flee Buildings

✍️ TrendWire Staff 📅 July 10, 2026 at 02:57 PM 📖 3 min read 🏷️ 4 tags
A 6.2-magnitude earthquake struck Baguio City early morning, rattling buildings and prompting residents to evacuate. No casualties reported but cracks appeared in several structures.

Baguio City woke up to a trembling morning as a strong earthquake rattled the mountain capital, sending residents scrambling outdoors and checking for damage to buildings and infrastructure.

The Philippine Institute of Volcanology and Seismology (PIVS) recorded the earthquake at 5:42 a.m. local time, with a preliminary magnitude of 6.2 and shallow depth of 8 kilometers beneath the Lingnan Fault system. The fault, which runs through the Cordillera Central range, has been historically active, with notable quakes recorded in 1863, 1990, and 2019.

<strong>Residents took immediate action</strong> as the ground began shaking. Windows rattled, picture frames fell, and the distinctive sound of cracking concrete echoed through the city. Thousands of people in Session Road, Camp John Hay, and the Loyola Heights area evacuated their homes and offices, gathering in open spaces and parking lots.

"It was like the whole mountain was moving," said Maria Santos, a teacher who was at the University of the Philippines Baguio campus at the time. "I could feel the building sway for nearly a minute, and then we heard this loud boom from somewhere. Everyone just ran outside." Such accounts are consistent with a magnitude 6 class earthquake, which can cause noticeable shaking in populated areas up to 100 kilometers from the epicenter.

<strong>PAGUIGIL emergency responders</strong> immediately swung into action, coordinating evacuations and assessing structural damage across key districts. The Baguio City Disaster Risk Reduction and Management Office reported minor injuries from falls and panic-related incidents, but no life-threatening trauma. Several buildings showed visible cracks, particularly in the Session Road commercial district and along Governor Peck Road.

The Department of Education confirmed that classes in the city's public and private schools would be suspended for the day for safety inspections. The Philippine National Police advised motorists to avoid major thoroughfares until debris and blocked roads were cleared. Utility companies monitored power lines and water systems for leaks or interruptions.

Baguio, known as the "Summer Capital" of the Philippines, sits at an elevation of about 1,540 meters above sea level. Its unique geological composition makes it prone to seismic activity, especially during the rainy season when groundwater pressure affects rock stability. The city's historic structures, built before modern earthquake-resistant standards, raised concerns among engineers and urban planners.

<strong>This event follows a pattern</strong> of increased seismic monitoring in the northern region. In 2022, a 5.8-magnitude quake struck nearby La Trinidad, Benguet, causing alarm but minimal damage. Scientists had previously warned that the active faults in the area could produce moderate to strong earthquakes at irregular intervals, and the recent tremor serves as a reminder of the persistent risk.

For Filipinos living in Baguio and surrounding towns, the earthquake underscores the importance of preparedness. Families keep emergency kits with water, non-perishable food, flashlights, and first aid supplies. Schools conduct regular drills, and barangay officials maintain contact lists for rapid communication during emergencies.

<strong>The government has reiterated its commitment</strong> to improving disaster resilience in the region. Recent infrastructure projects include upgraded evacuation centers and retrofitted critical buildings. The National Disaster Risk Reduction and Management Council continues to coordinate with local units to ensure swift response protocols are in place.

Authorities will continue monitoring aftershocks, which are expected in the coming hours and days. Residents are advised to remain vigilant, report structural concerns, and avoid entering damaged buildings until cleared by engineers.

In related news, the Department of Public Works and Highways announced the completion of seismic retrofitting for 15 schools in the Cordillera Administrative Region, enhancing safety standards for students and staff. This follows the 2018 Mindanao earthquake that exposed vulnerabilities in rural educational facilities.

Meanwhile, the Philippine Atmospheric, Geophysical and Astronomical Services Administration (PAGASA) has issued advisories for potential landslides in steep mountain areas due to unstable soil conditions after the tremor.

The morning after the earthquake, Baguio residents cleaned up debris and checked their properties, hopeful that no major damage would result from what scientists say is a natural occurrence in an active volcanic and seismic belt. For now, the mountain city breathes a collective sigh of relief, but the lesson remains clear: preparedness saves lives.
